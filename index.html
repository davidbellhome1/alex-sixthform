Three routes. One Alex.

Three sensible ways through Gordon's Sixth Form for the things you've told us you want. Plus the coursework-to-exam balance for each one.

You want to run your own water park one day. Maybe work with animals, possibly at Disney's Animal Kingdom. You don't want a desk job. Drama matters to you. Business is your strongest subject. You and Matthew are setting up a Whatnot reselling business.

We looked hard at BTEC Sport. It's probably too much sport and not enough leisure management. Seven of nine units are sport content, so it's off the list.

That leaves three options below. Each one points at university or an apprenticeship, just by different routes. Read them and tell us what you think.

How Gordon's actually picks subjects

Three A Levels, one from each of three timetable blocks (A, B, C, D). Plus one "+1" supplementary option from block E in Year 12 only.

Block A
Maths, Chemistry, Economics, Sociology, English Lit, PE, Art, Photography, Biology
Block B
Maths, Biology, Chemistry, Product Design, Psychology, Business, History, Media
Block C
Physics, Geography, Economics, English Language, Music, English Lit, Spanish/German/French
Block D
Physics, STEM, Psychology, Computer Science, History, Media, Drama, BTEC Business

Subjects in bold are in your shortlist. The rule that matters: only one subject per block. Drama only sits in D, Geography only in C, Business only in B, so a lot of the combinations build themselves.

Drama: 60% practical, devising a piece, performing or designing live to a visiting examiner. Geography: 4 days of fieldwork over two years plus a 4,000-word independent investigation you design yourself. EPQ: 100% your own project, your own topic, your own deadlines.

Built around the water park ambition. Business is your strongest subject, so keep going with it. Geography matters more than it looks for leisure businesses: site, demographics, weather, planning, environment. Drama keeps the performance and presentation side live. If you're running customer-facing businesses one day, that's a muscle you'll use a lot.

EPQ topic suggests itself: a feasibility study for a UK family water park.

Doors this opens
Apprenticeships at Center Parcs, Merlin, IHG, Whitbread. Business management, hospitality, leisure management, events management or property development degrees if you go to uni. Most non-STEM routes either way.
Doors this closes
Direct entry to a science degree from school. Zoology, animal behaviour or marine biology direct at 18 isn't reachable from this stack. Those are still reachable later via a Level 3 Animal Management BTEC, a zookeeper apprenticeship, or a university foundation year, just with an extra year or two of route-building.

Hands-on / applied work
Biology: 12 required practicals (lab work) but assessed through the written exams, not separate coursework. Geography: 4 days of fieldwork plus the 4,000-word investigation. EPQ: independent project. Heaviest exam load of the three options.

This is the route if animals are central to what you want to do, and if you might want a science degree in zoology, animal behaviour, marine biology or conservation as the way in. Biology A Level is the cleanest route into those degrees direct from school.

Worth being clear though. Biology isn't the only way into hands-on animal work. Zookeeper and animal-attraction jobs typically need 5 GCSEs plus a Level 3 animal qualification (a specialist Animal Management BTEC at somewhere like Sparsholt or Plumpton, or an apprenticeship at a zoo). And if you finished sixth form without Biology and then decided you wanted a zoology degree, several universities (Plymouth, Derby, others) offer foundation-year routes specifically for that. So this isn't "Biology or never work with animals." It's "Biology is the most direct route, and the only stack at Gordon's that keeps the science-degree path open from age 18."

Trade-off: Drama drops as a qualification. You'd still do school productions and the band, just not for the A Level.

Doors this opens
Direct entry to zoology, animal behaviour, marine biology, conservation or wildlife management degrees. Vet nursing routes. The science and research end of the animal world, including Disney's professional animal-programs internships, which want zoology/biology-related majors.
Doors this closes
Drama. The stack still works fine for the water park / leisure route through Business and Geography, you'd just be presenting a different story.

Hands-on / applied work
BTEC Business: coursework-led with one exam (Finance) and one supervised marketing task. Portfolio of real assignments. Media Studies: 30% non-exam, you actually make media products (film, magazine, website) to a brief. Geography: 4 days of fieldwork plus 4,000-word investigation. EPQ: independent project. Most practical stack of the three.

The version with the most coursework and the fewest exams. BTEC Business is more portfolio than A Level Business. Media Studies you've said you'd like. It covers how stories, brands and audiences work, which is directly useful for marketing a leisure venue one day. Half exam, half making things.

It's also a slightly lower bar on grade requirements, so if Year 11 doesn't quite go to plan, this stack stays open more easily.

Drama drops here too. This stack points toward apprenticeships and applied degrees rather than traditional academic ones.

If Media doesn't appeal, Psychology fits the same Block B slot. Psychology is 100% exam though, so swapping it in shifts the stack back toward 67% exam / 33% coursework.

Doors this opens
Most leisure, hospitality, business, events and marketing apprenticeships. Direct entry to industry at 18. Applied degrees in business management, leisure, hospitality, marketing or media if you go the university route.
Doors this closes
Drama. The most academically competitive universities. Any science route. The vet/zoology side.

Picture the two years

Which Tuesday in Year 12 sounds most like you?

The destinations aren't that different. Most things stay reachable from all three. What really changes is the day-to-day shape of the next two years. Read these three Tuesdays and notice which one you'd actually want to be in.

Tuesday in Option 01

Business, fieldwork, stage

Morning: Business lesson on global marketing case studies. Notes, discussion, working through past paper answers. After lunch: Drama rehearsal for the devised piece (60% of the A Level). Block work, character development, designing your section. Evening prep: reading the Geography textbook on regenerating places, sketching essay plans for tomorrow.
Mix of academic and stage. Performance work keeps you out of the chair for at least one block of the day.
Tuesday in Option 02

Lab, library, fieldwork data

Morning: Biology practical on enzyme kinetics in the lab, writing up the results. After lunch: Business lesson, same as Option 01. Evening prep: revising Biology Topic 3, analysing fieldwork data in Excel for the Geography investigation.
Heavy on science and reading. Lots of exam revision sitting underneath everything. The most academic of the three.
Tuesday in Option 03

Assignments, edit suite, projects

Morning: BTEC Business coursework session, working on your Exploring Business assignment. After lunch: Media Studies in the edit suite, cutting together your cross-media production piece. Evening prep: Geography fieldwork write-up, sorting photos from the coast trip.
Coursework deadlines and project work rather than revision. Most of what you do has something to show at the end.

Three things to weight

Once you've pictured the Tuesdays, the choice comes down to how much each of these three things matters to you. Rank them in your head:

Do you want to keep performing as part of your school week? How much exam pressure versus coursework feels right? Do you want the science-degree route open direct from school at 18?

If performing tops the list, that's Option 01. Drama only sits in one timetable block at Gordon's, and dropping it means dropping it. Hands-on animal work is still reachable later via a Level 3 Animal Management BTEC at Sparsholt or Plumpton, or a zookeeper apprenticeship, just with an extra step after sixth form.

If keeping the science degree route open tops the list, that's Option 02. Biology A Level is the cleanest way into zoology, animal behaviour or marine biology direct from school. Heaviest exam load of the three, and Drama goes.

If making things and lighter exam pressure tops the list, that's Option 03. Half coursework, half exam, with Media production work and BTEC portfolio assignments instead of three full A Level exam loads. Also a slightly lower grade bar, so it stays open more easily if Year 11 doesn't quite go to plan.
